By June 2025, nearly everyone working in software has used AI tools to some extent. I’m fortunate to work at a company that aggressively adopts new AI solutions to boost efficiency and deliver more value to our customers. Today, over 80% of our unit tests and 50% of our code are completed by AI agents. Nearly all of our documentation is either drafted or reviewed by AI before being shared for feedback. AI has become an indispensable part of our workflow and we’ve even slowed down hiring because, with AI’s help, our existing team can accomplish so much more.

Having spent over a decade in ML and software development, I’ve seen how new tools can improve productivity. But this is the first time I’m genuinely questioning whether these tools are better than I am, even in areas where I’ve always excelled.

After reading others’ experiences and speaking with friends and colleagues, my current perspective is this:In an age where AI can generate code and draft documents in seconds, the role of the human engineer isn’t disappearing , but it’s evolving into something different.

### The New Shape of Engineering

AI is transforming the landscape of software development. It’s very impressive to see someone who has zero coding experience can use tools like Vibe Coding or code agents to define a problem space and allowing AI agents to explore, experiment, and iteratively arrive at an end-to-end solution. This makes the communication between business stakeholders and engineers become way more simpler than before. If an AI can handle the “how” of building software, what is left for us to do?

The answer is simple: everything that matters most.

While AI excels at executing well-defined tasks, the enduring value of human engineers lies in the skills that are difficult to digitize: strategic thinking, architectural taste, social intelligence, and true innovation. In the long run, our role will shift from being mere builders to becoming the architects, strategists, and conductors of technology.

### The Strategist: Seeing the Big Picture

Before a single line of code is written, someone must define the “why.” AI is brilliant at solving the problems it’s given, but humans excel at identifying and framing the right problems to tackle in the first place.

This role requires a deep understanding of business goals, market needs, and strategic priorities. Human engineers can weigh competing factors , like budget constraints, time-to-market, and long-term vision — to make informed decisions. They connect technical solutions to business imperatives, ensuring that what’s being built delivers real value.

Much of this critical context isn’t stored in any knowledge base. It often comes from informal conversations, asking thought-provoking questions, and understanding the company’s current priorities. Framing the right problem still relies on human intuition, curiosity, and experience.

### The Architect: Cultivating a “Taste” for Great Solutions

Experienced engineers develop something that can only be described as “taste” , an intuitive, experience-driven sense of what makes a system not just functional, but elegant, maintainable, and scalable.

This isn’t just about aesthetics, it’s about architectural vision. It’s the ability to design with the future in mind, anticipating changing needs and building in flexibility from the start. Every large-scale project involves navigating complex trade-offs , balancing performance, cost, security, and the developer experience. An engineer’s “taste” is their seasoned judgment, honed over years of seeing different solutions succeed and fail. It’s what enables them to strike the optimal balance for any given situation.

### The Diplomat: “Reading the Air” in a Team Sport

Software engineering has always been a profoundly social and collaborative discipline. The success of large-scale projects often hinges on the quality of human interaction , something AI simply can’t replicate.

Engineers need to negotiate requirements with stakeholders, persuade teammates to embrace new technical directions, mentor junior developers, and resolve conflicts as they arise. These responsibilities demand empathy, emotional intelligence, and a keen awareness of team dynamics. The ability to navigate an organization’s social and political landscape , to “read the air” — is an invaluable human skill. It’s what ensures projects move forward smoothly, especially when challenges or disagreements emerge.

### The Innovator: From Pattern-Matching to True Breakthroughs

AI excels at pattern-matching, generating creative solutions within the boundaries of what it has already seen. But true, paradigm-shifting innovation often comes from connecting seemingly unrelated ideas and asking, “What if?”

This kind of out-of-the-box thinking is uniquely human (so far) and it’s the spark behind genuine technological breakthroughs. Human creativity fuels progress in ways that algorithms, no matter how advanced, simply can’t replicate.

Additionally, humans play a critical role as ethical stewards. We must consider the broader societal impact of the technologies we create and make value-driven decisions that are — and must remain — beyond the reach of any algorithm.

### The Future is a Partnership

We don’t try to outrun cars — we drive them. The same logic applies to working with AI. Rather than competing, the real power comes from learning how to partner with these tools.

The rise of AI doesn’t mean the end of human engineers. Instead, it gives us a chance to offload rote, repetitive tasks, freeing us to focus on what we do best. AI will become the ultimate tool, handling the “how” so that we can concentrate on the “what” and the “why.”

The engineer of the future will be less of a coder and more of a conductor, masterfully orchestrating powerful AI tools, guiding projects with strategic vision, and infusing technology with the creativity, judgment, and humanity it needs.
